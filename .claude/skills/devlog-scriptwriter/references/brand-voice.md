# Brand Voice: Pavlo (Devlog Scriptwriter)

## Persona

Pavlo is a programmer building his first game -- a UE5 Action RPG about a Troll who guards a bridge. He's great at code but openly terrible at art, and he documents the messy, real process of learning game dev. His videos are short, visual-first stories where the gameplay does the talking and the voiceover adds context, humor, and honesty. He's not a teacher -- he's a guy figuring things out and sharing the fun parts.

## Tone Dimensions

### Self-deprecating humor
Pavlo laughs at himself, not others. He's the butt of the joke.

- "I'm pretty good at programming but when it comes to art I'm absolutely terrible" (#1)
- "Yeah... it was easier with the crates" (#6)
- "okay... a bit too strong" (#3) -- the physics breaking is funny because it's HIS fault
- "The process is fun and painful at the same time, like everything in gamedev" (#5)

**Boundaries:** No fixed limits -- humor is context-dependent and story-dependent. Pavlo is open to anything that fits the video's context. The pattern from transcripts holds: humor naturally gravitates toward the work (bugs, physics, mistakes) because that's what the videos show.

### Honest about struggles
No pretending everything works first try. The mess IS the content.

- "I'm new to all of this" (#3)
- "time to fix this" (#2) -- said casually, not dramatically
- "if you do it wrong you get this" (#5) -- shows the broken result

### Understated excitement
When things work, the reaction is quiet satisfaction, not hype.

- "every small thing that works feels like a little victory" (#3) -- not "THIS IS INCREDIBLE"
- "it finally feels better" (#3) -- not "IT LOOKS AMAZING"
- "this is the most fun mechanic I've made so far" (#4) -- specific, comparative, not absolute

### Forward momentum
Almost every video ends by teasing what's next. Never a formal goodbye.

- "let's find something that fights back next" (#3)
- "maybe it becomes the next" (#4)
- "But the best projectile... is the one that bites back" (#6) -- ending IS the tease

### Casual technicality
Technical terms appear but get immediately simplified.

- "physics asset -- it's basically the instruction how each body part can move when it gets hit" (#5)
- "the throw impulse was applied to just one body part, creating rotational chaos" (#6) -- then immediately "So instead of pushing one bone, let's push the whole body"
- "cut into chunks in the fracture tool" (#3) -- no further explanation needed because the VISUAL shows it

**Technical depth level:** Accessible to everyone, not just developers. Think vedinad-style -- a general audience should understand the content. Video #5's deeper technical explanation is fine as long as the visual shows what's happening and the voiceover keeps it simple. Default to the #3/#6 level (one technical sentence + immediate plain English). The channel is young -- open to experimenting with depth.

## Sentence Rules

| Rule | Evidence | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| Maximum ~15 words per sentence | Longest sentences across all videos are around 15 words | "The first time I saw ragdoll physics was Max Payne 2 and back in 2003 that felt mind-blowing" (#5) is the outlier at ~18 words |
| Fragments are fine | Used in every video | "And every Troll needs something to smash." "Time to fix this." |
| No subordinate clauses | Zero instances of "which," "whereas," "although" in 6 videos | Pavlo uses "and," "but," "so" -- never complex connectors |
| Technical term + immediate simple explanation | When tech terms appear, next phrase is plain English | "physics asset -- it's basically the instruction how each body part can move" |
| Natural pauses are authentic | "okay...", "um...", "..." pauses appear in natural speech | "Okay. So now... um... okay, let's try again" (#3) |
| Start sentences with "And," "But," "So" | Used constantly as connectors | "And every Troll needs something to smash" (#3), "But smashing boxes is only fun as an extra" (#3), "So instead of pushing one bone" (#6) |
| No formal transitions | Zero "however," "furthermore," "additionally," "moreover" | Transitions are "and," "but," "so," "okay," or just a new sentence |
| Contractions always | "I'm," "don't," "can't," "let's," "he's," "shouldn't" | Never "I am," "do not," "cannot" |

## Vocabulary Rules

**Verbs:** make, add, fix, throw, hit, grab, push, drop, try, break, smash, pick up, crank up, split, cut
**Adjectives:** fun, strong, better, basic, real, readable, realistic, mind-blowing (once, for nostalgia)

The following words/phrases appear ZERO times across all 6 transcripts. AI tools commonly generate them. Pavlo confirmed this list -- no additions needed. Open to moderate experimentation within the brand spirit. This is a living document: update when new patterns emerge.

- leverage, utilize, harness, optimize, elevate, empower

## Off-Brand Example

The same content rewritten in generic AI style -- showing what to AVOID:

> In today's exciting devlog update, I'm diving into one of the most challenging aspects of game development: ragdoll physics and enemy throwing mechanics!
>
> I've always wanted to implement a feature where the Troll can grab enemies and hurl them across the battlefield. The journey to get this working was incredible.
>
> The initial implementation presented an interesting challenge. When applying force to a single bone in the physics asset, the resulting motion created an unrealistic rotational effect. After extensive testing and iteration, I discovered that distributing the impulse across multiple bones simultaneously produced much more satisfying results.
>
> This is a perfect example of the classic game development trade-off between realism and gameplay feel. Sometimes you have to sacrifice physical accuracy to create something that's truly fun to play!

**Why it fails:** "Exciting devlog update" (hype). "Diving into" (AI slop). "The journey" (AI slop). "Incredible" (inflated). "Interesting challenge" (vague). "Extensive testing and iteration" (generic). "Satisfying results" (AI completion phrase). "Perfect example" (over-explains). Exclamation mark at end (fake energy). Long sentences. Formal structure. Tells instead of shows. No personality.

## Voice Checklist (Use Before Finalizing Any Script)

- [ ] Does it open with a story/situation, not an announcement?
- [ ] Are sentences under 15 words on average?
- [ ] Does it use at least one Pavlo signature phrase naturally?
- [ ] Is there a specific detail (number, tool name, game reference)?
- [ ] Does the voiceover describe what IS on screen, not what isn't?
- [ ] Would Pavlo actually say this out loud without cringing?
- [ ] Does it end with forward momentum (tease, question, surprise)?
- [ ] Zero banned words from the NEVER USE list?
- [ ] Could a viewer tell this is Pavlo and not any other gamedev channel?
